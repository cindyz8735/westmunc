Background Guide

 

 

The Human Rights Council (HRC) of West Munc is a committee of the UN General Assembly that deals with the protection of human rights on a both a country specific and global scale across the international community. This year, the HRC will explore the topic of the violation of rights of indigenous and native people. Members of the HRC will gather with other nations to draft a resolution regarding the following issues indigenous people face. 1) Health care access and support and 2) Violence over natural resources.

Low standards of health remains a prominent issue that indigenous people face due to poverty, overcrowding, and environmental contamination. Diseases such as HIV prevail among communities of indigenous people at a disproportionately higher rate. However, with governmental neglectance, clinical care remains poor and inadequate. Worldwide, indigenous people endure violence in conflict areas over exploitation of natural resources. In Colombia, indigenous people have faced severe impacts and losses from the violence between rebel groups and governmental forces. In areas of conflict minerals such as Congo, indigenous natives face environmental destruction and displacement as well.

Caroline Cosovich

Mitchell Pon

Meiling Thompson

 

Background Guide

 

 

Countries in Europe and the European Union have been fighting against terrorism for many years. The most prominent threat to the EU is the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS), which has claimed responsibility for several attacks throughout the European Union and its associated countries in the last few years, including the November 2015 Paris attacks, March 2016 Brussels attacks, and the June 2016 Istanbul Airport bombings. The influence carried by this radical Islamic group compromises the domestic security of many countries and threatens to decimate their livelihoods. Proper, effective legislation on what to do in the case of a terrorist attack and how to prevent ones in the future is highly necessary to citizens and governments within the EU.

This committee will also connect the issue of domestic terrorism in the EU to that of the Syrian Refugee Crisis. Recently, the link between the two has been problematic enough that some terrorist attacks have been perpetrated by refugees who are discontent and therefore commit to destruction alongside the Islamic State. Since the outbreak of civil war in Syria in 2011, about 11 million Syrians have fled the country and about 6.6 million remain internally displaced in Syria. About 1 million refugees have applied for asylum within the EU, with Germany and Sweden taking in the most refugees. However, Syrian refugees have recently come under suspicion after ISIS claimed to be hiding militants among the refugees settling in the EU. In this committee, representatives will attempt to dilute the connection between the Syrian Refugee Crisis and the threat of possible ISIS attacks.

Background Guide

 

 

Our committee will be convening on June 28, 1914. The heir to the throne of the Austro-Hungarian empire, Archduke Franz Ferdinand, and his wife Sophie had just been killed by young Serbian nationalist Gavrilo Princip. Consequently, the Allies and the Central Powers have each called emergency meetings to discuss negotiations with the other side and eventually war (this is the start of our simulation). Tensions between European powers had already been building for decades, and historically the assassination of the Archduke was the event that sparked World War I. Alliances, militarism, technological advancement, imperialism, and nationalism all helped create an atmosphere prime for one of the deadliest wars in the modern era.

This committee will be run as a joint crisis, meaning that there will be two separate committee rooms working through a crisis simulation of World War I from its beginning to its end. One committee will be the Central Powers made up of Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, and Bulgaria. The other committee will be the Allies made up of the United Kingdom, France, the United States, Belgium, Italy, Russia, Japan and Serbia. The course and eventual outcome of the war depends on the decisions delegates make.
